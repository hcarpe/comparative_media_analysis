Limetown and My Father's Long, Long Legs: Using sound to articulate postmodern digital Gothic horror

Hope Carpenter


Introduction

In his article “Gothic Digital Technologies,” Joseph Crawford defines the “digital Gothic” as media that makes use of the “technological possibilities of online media to find new ways of constructing and distributing Gothic narratives” (Crawford 72). Both podcast Limetown and Twine game My Father’s Long, Long Legs utilize aspects of late postmodern horror and the digital Gothic to create a unique digital horror experience.

In my comparative media analysis, I will be comparing my chosen works more broadly but also comparing more specifically how these works create their respective horror experiences through sound.


Limetown

In their article “Are you sitting (un)comfortably? Sound, horror and radio,” Richard J. Hand declares that mid-20th century radio horror “remains one of the richest eras in popular horror culture” (Sitting 49). I will be looking at Limetown through the lens of self-reflexivity and metalepsis.

Limetown is a podcast released in 2015 by Two-Up Productions. In the first season, American Public Radio reporter Lia Haddock explores exactly what happened during The Panic on the night of February 8, 2004, in Limetown, Tennessee when 327 men, women, and children disappeared and haven’t been heard from since. After publishing the first episode, Lia is contacted by a string of Limetown survivors, each interview ending with more questions than answers. Limetown is revealed to be a front for a secret scientific experiment determining whether humans can possibly partake in mind-to-mind contact via a chip implanted in someone’s brain. Season one ends with Lia meeting Lenore Dougal, the double-agent hired to convince the orchestrator of Limetown to sell the project to another company, who reveals exactly what happened during The Panic and after. The second season, released in 2018, begins five days after Lia has been abducted mid-interview with Lenore Dougal. The podcast was successful, becoming the number one podcast in the US in under 2 months, and Limetown was eventually adapted to become a TV show on the Peacock streaming service in 2019. For this project, I will be focusing only on the podcast’s first season.

Crawford notes that in "traditional ‘found document’ or ‘found footage’ horror fiction, the action is over by the time the audience arrives, the author or filmmaker is dead (or worse), and all that remains is to go through the films or papers they have left behind in order to discover what happened to them” (Crawford 75). Limetown does have found footage embedded into the narrative, but the footage isn’t "found" in a traditional sense. Instead of stumbling across footage such as audiotapes of the 911 call from Limetown requesting emergency services and other tapes of The Panic, Lia finds the footage through research and as gifts through the connections she makes with Limetown survivors.


Listen to the first few minutes of the first episode of season 1 of Limestone to hear clips of some of the audio files Lia finds through research.

Limetown is self-reflexive in its presentation of found footage because of the unorthodox ways in which Lia comes into possession of the Limetown footage. In more traditional forms of ‘found footage’ media, characters are not intentionally searching for the footage that is found, the most popular example being The Blair Witch Project in which the students’ footage is unexpectedly “found” by police searching for the missing students. In a stunning twist of events in the last episode of the first season, Lia’s own podcast becomes found footage when the podcast is dramatically cut off from its live broadcast and Lia is found missing from the scene of the taping.

In their “Sound, horror and radio” article, Hand describes how off-stage sounds in plays in the early 20th century served to “paint a picture of the world beyond the confines of the … tiny, claustrophobic stage as well as enhanc[e] the mood of the story,” (Sitting 12). Hand goes on to describe that while stage actors were more melodramatic, voice actors in radio dramas of the time created “a more natural, intimate style” and experience for the audience (Sitting 33). Even today, podcasts and radio shows appear more intimate than plays by sounding more natural and conversational.

One of the ways in which radio and podcasts make their recordings so natural is through outside sound effects. Whenever Lia is conducting an interview, the audience is made keenly aware of what is going on around the interview through sound effects.


The audience can hear sounds of nature in the background while Lia is interviewing Limetown survivor Deirdre Wells at a park.

In another one of their articles (coincidentally from the same book appropriately titled Listen in Terror) “Multifarious terrors: horror audio in the digital age,” Richard J. Hand notes that “not only has high-quality location recording become easier to realise for the producer, but the concept of location has also become a more important consideration for the listener” (horror audio 191). Limetown knows how important location has become for an audience, so when Lia is outside we hear birds chirping and when she is by the ocean we hear the waves. We even hear the first Limetown survivor to come forward, Winona, making tea in the background of her interview. This metalepsis serves the purpose of transgressing boundaries inside and outside of the text, the text being the podcast, in order to humanize the fictional by relating it to our own soundscapes.


My Father's Long, Long Legs

In addition to audio horror, Radio shows have evolved to allow audience choice. Crawford notes that, in radio, “the listener will get an enhanced experience if he or she is actively involved in the creative process, whether that is by directly influencing the storyline or by contributing a large dose of imagination to the production”. By the twenty-first century, audiences have been required to not only listen but “physically engage, to take control and even some responsibility in shaping the unfurling narrative,” by voting for contestants on shows such as American Idol and Big Brother (Crawford 197).

One engaging feature of Gothic digital technologies that Crawford discusses is the “horrible hyperlink,” which “effectively dares the reader to click on it despite their knowledge that it might lead to something awful” (Crawford 76). The hyperlink function brings to mind Twine, an open-source software that allows users to create custom, choose your own adventure story-driven games on their computer. One such story is My Father’s Long, Long Legs, an “interactive horror story about family, unease, and loss” (ztul). In the game, the protagonist describes growing up in a midwestern house with a dirt basement. Their father slowly retreats deeper and deeper into the basement as he digs tunnels downward in order to return to humanity’s “original glory”. Their mother eventually leaves their father and takes the protagonist and their brother with her; the protagonist returns years later alongside their brother to check on their father, and the protagonist loses their brother in the basement after being scared of what their father has become and fleeing. When the protagonist is in the basement searching for their father and brother, the player’s mouse becomes a flashlight and only a small radius of the page is visible at a time.


Here you can see the mouse turn into a flashlight as a player clicks through the game.

The limited visibility the game maker affords the game player becomes a visual “horrible hyperlink,” as the player feels like something could suddenly flash into the flashlight’s field of view at any given moment.

When the protagonist is in the basement, the audience can hear the sound of their father’s shovel routinely shoveling dirt. After the protagonist flees, a picture of their father’s long, long legs flash on the screen, designed to surprise the player. At one point, the screen goes blank except for the flashlight's light and audio of their father humming a tune is played as well, evoking Mark Fisher’s notion of the eerie we discussed in class.


Multiple audio and visual elements come into play at the end of My Father's Long, Long Legs.

Because the eerie is “constituted by a failure of absence or by a failure of presence” and their father fails to appear until the end—and even when he does appear, it is just his legs from feet to knee—this scene is eerie because his voice is present in the absence of a body (Fisher 60).

Crawford notes that the digital Gothic “takes advantage of the way in which webpages tend to be read.” Since most users scroll reflexively as they read, by the time they reach anything scary it has already appeared on-screen and is unavoidable to the eye. This is different from a book, where readers can choose whether they want to deliberately turn a page to see the horror they know (or think they know) is coming. “Online, such material can ‘creep up on’ the reader, appearing onscreen almost before they realize it is there” (Crawford 79). This is certainly true in My Father’s Long, Long Legs, as the image automatically appears on the screen without the user having to do anything at all.

Hand connects the idea of listeners gaining “greater freedom to access, acquire or download material” and online digital Gothic interactivity to gaming, noting that “the decisions and actions of the player can have a direct impact on the game and its narrative, creating a sense of choice, even if such choices may be illusory to different degrees” (horror audio 192, 193). Twine connects both interactivity and choice. The Twine interface also helps authors utilize hypermediation by employing a distinct interface for users to play on that is unlike any other game.


Synthesis

Although Crawford identifies a common feature between online horror websites to be their “collaborative nature” with their audience, neither Limetown nor My Father’s Long, Long Legs collaborate with their audiences about production or narrative in a noteworthy way (Crawford 78). More traditional Gothic elements include a big reveal of past secrets resurfacing, and while the truth of what really happened in Limetown is the past secret that is revealed at the end of the podcast’s first season, My Father’s Long, Long Legs does not appear to have a reveal of the same caliber. The father’s legs are revealed, but no hint as to why the protagonist’s father is digging in their basement is ever given except for the protagonist’s uncle telling the father that he has "done very well for himself, all things considered" (ztul).

A lot has changed in the world of online horror since the internet was first invented, and as “old barriers between traditional horror media (copyrighted, single-author, for-profit, overtly fictional) and online horror media (viral, anonymous, crowdsourced, non-commercial, often blurring the lines between fact and fiction) have increasingly broken down … some creators of online horror fiction have become more professional, while others have retreated deeper into anonymity” (Crawford 82, 83). The creators of Limetown have taken a more professional approach by releasing a professionally produced podcast onto major streaming platforms such as Spotify and Apple Podcasts. Twine is a much lesser-known tool, and as a result, My Father’s Long, Long Legs, on the other hand, has taken a more anonymous approach. The game is posted on itch.io, “an open marketplace for independent digital creators with a focus on independent video games.” Itch.io has become “a collection of some of the most unique, interesting, and independent creations you'll find on the web,” but the website is in no way, shape, or form mainstream (itch.io).


A screenshot of the itch.io "About" page, showing that the website positions itself as an indie web game service.

In their “Sound, horror and radio” chapter, Hand asserts that “sound can … invisibly manipulate our mood” and is “extremely hard to avoid” (sitting 8). Both Limetown and My Father’s Long, Long Legs use sound effects to elicit a response in their audience. Limetown does use sound more often to elicit a response from the audience, but it is important to note that Limetown is dependent upon sounds more than My father’s Long, Long Legs because a podcast is only audio and thus cannot rely on any visual components.


TW: GUNSHOT
At the end of the fourth episode of Limetown's first season, Limetown survivor Max Finlayson is presumably shot and killed while on the phone with Lia. The sound of the gunshot is harrowing and haunting, especially if a listener does not expect it. The background noise makes the clip even more haunting.

Hand goes on to say that “the ‘intangible’ essence of sound – in other words, its invisibility – gives it a fluid and emotional quality that enables its usage to shift from the blatant to the latent” (sitting 10). At one point or another, both Limetown and My Father’s Long, Long Legs are “structured by the language of sound, making us hear and thus feel the environment,” and this notion of sound lying dormant until needed elicits Fisher’s notion of the eerie as well as Hand’s notion of location recording (sitting 15). Both the presence and absence of sound can be used to elicit horror in an audience; while sound can be used to indicate location and the eerie, it can also be taken away in order to put an audience on edge as well.


Conclusion

Both Limetown and My Father’s Long, Long Legs incorporate aspects of the late postmodern horror and digital Gothic experience into their stories. Despite their differences, both works uniquely use sound in order to create a horror experience in the absence of more traditional visual effects.
